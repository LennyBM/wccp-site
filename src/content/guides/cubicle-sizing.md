## Why cubicle dimensions matter

A dairy cow lies down for twelve to fourteen hours a day. If the cubicle is too short, she'll perch with her hind legs in the passage and dung on the bed. If it's too narrow, she'll bruise her hocks against the divider. If the kerb is too high, she'll catch her hooves climbing in.

The result, in every case: more lameness, more mastitis, less milk. A well-sized cubicle pays for itself inside a lactation.

This guide gives you the numbers. They're drawn from AHDB Dairy's *Cow Comfort* guidance, the RUMA welfare standards, and thirty years of casting cubicles for West Country dairies.

## Sizing by breed and liveweight

Cubicle dimensions follow liveweight, not breed name. A pedigree Holstein at 750kg needs more space than a smaller commercial cross at 600kg. Use the table below as your starting point and adjust to your herd's actual weights.

| Breed / type            | Liveweight | Lying length | Divider centres | Kerb height | Brisket distance |
|-------------------------|-----------:|-------------:|----------------:|------------:|-----------------:|
| Jersey                  |    450-550kg |        2.20m |           1.10m |       200mm |            1.50m |
| Friesian / cross        |    550-650kg |        2.30m |           1.15m |       250mm |            1.60m |
| Holstein-Friesian       |    650-750kg |        2.40m |           1.20m |       250mm |            1.70m |
| Pedigree Holstein       |    750-850kg |        2.50m |           1.25m |       250mm |            1.75m |
| Beef / suckler          |    600-800kg |        2.60m |           1.25m |       300mm |            1.70m |

These are clear internal dimensions, not centre-to-centre — measure from the rear kerb to the head rail.

## The brisket board: the bit most people get wrong

The brisket board is the bar or precast block that stops the cow lunging too far forward. Set it correctly and she lies fully in the cubicle. Set it too close and she perches; too far and she lies forward and dungs on the bed.

The rule: brisket distance equals lying length minus 700mm. For a 2.4m Holstein cubicle, that's 1.70m from the rear kerb to the front face of the brisket. For a 2.2m Jersey, it's 1.50m.

## Divider design and clearance

Standard precast dividers come in two profiles:

**Loop dividers** are a single curved bar. They're the cheapest, easiest to retro-fit, and offer the most freedom of movement. Best for established herds that already use cubicles well.

**Cantilever dividers** are wall-mounted, with no front leg. They give the cow a completely clear lunging space — important if she's coming from a yard or straw system and isn't yet confident with cubicles.

Whatever profile you choose, the divider should sit 200mm above the bed at the loin and 1.10m above the kerb at the head. The cow needs to be able to swing her head sideways without catching the divider.

## Kerb design and the hock-injury question

Cows step into cubicles by lifting one front hoof, then the other. A 250mm kerb is comfortable — they barely notice it. A 350mm kerb is a daily strain and shows up as hock thickening within a season.

Lower kerbs (200mm) reduce that risk but mean dung gets onto the bed more often. The right answer for most West Country dairies, with twice-daily scrape-throughs, is a 250mm precast kerb with a 50mm fall toward the passage.

## Lying surface — concrete, mat or mattress?

Bare concrete is a non-starter for welfare. RUMA's standards require a "soft, non-slip lying surface", which in practice means either:

- A 25mm rubber mat (cheapest, replace every 5-7 years)
- A mattress with crumb-rubber filling (mid-tier, 10-year life)
- A waterbed (premium, 15-year life, best for lameness reduction)

## Cubicle numbers and herd dynamics

Plan one cubicle per cow plus 5% — so a 200-cow herd needs at least 210 cubicles. The extras absorb the refusals: cows lower in the dominance hierarchy will sometimes wait for a "preferred" cubicle rather than use one near a bullying neighbour.

If you can stretch to 1.10 cubicles per cow, lying time goes up measurably and you'll see fewer slip-ups in the passage. AHDB's research from the 2018-2022 lameness trials shows a 0.4 percentage-point lameness reduction per 0.05 cubicles-per-cow added.

## Layout: rows of two, three, or four?

Two rows back-to-back ("head-to-head") with a central feed passage is the standard West Country layout. Three rows give you better cow flow but need a wider shed footprint. Four rows are typical for 300+ cow herds and need careful ventilation design — get an engineer in.

For any layout, stick to a 3.0-3.5m feed passage and a 2.4-2.7m scrape passage. Anything narrower and dominant cows will block traffic.

## Common sizing mistakes we see

After thirty years on the road we keep seeing the same three errors. First, sizing for the average cow rather than the largest 10%. Second, brisket boards set by builder's eye rather than tape measure. Third, no fall on the cubicle bed, so the bedding ends up at the back end of the cubicle within a fortnight.
