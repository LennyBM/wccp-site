## The brief

Robert Eddy at Trewartha Farm runs a 240-cow pedigree Holstein dairy on the Hayle estuary. In autumn 2024 he secured Slurry Infrastructure Grant Round 3 funding for a major expansion: a new 60-cubicle extension, replacement of the original 1980s cubicle dividers across the main shed, and a brand-new above-ground slurry store sized for six months' storage plus 25% headroom.

He came to WCCP through a recommendation from his neighbouring estate, who'd had us cast a retaining wall job two years earlier. The brief was simple: SSAFO-compliant slurry, AHDB-compliant cubicles, and finish before the cows came in for winter.

## The challenge

Three things made the job tight. First, the SIG paperwork required capacity calculations and exposure-class certificates submitted before any concrete was poured. Second, the existing cubicle shed was occupied by milking cows during the changeover — we had to stage the divider replacement section by section, working in 30-cubicle blocks during the day and letting the cows back in overnight. Third, the access track to the slurry store site ran past the parlour and couldn't take more than 18 tonnes during milking hours.

## What we delivered

We cast 252 cubicle dividers (2.4m Holstein lying length, cantilever profile) and 84m of matching kerb at the Holsworthy yard between September and October 2025. The slurry panels — 36 units at 3.6m height, 180mm thickness, sulphate-resisting C40/50 — were poured in a single batch run to keep the mix consistent across the store.

Our install crew spent four weeks on site between October and November. The cubicle replacement was done in three rotations, each completed during a single morning so the herd was back in by 4pm milking. The slurry store base was poured by Robert's regular groundworker; we delivered the panels in three loads, stitched the joints with bentonite waterstop, and topped them out with a precast capping beam.

## The result

The new slurry store passed its SSAFO inspection on first visit, with the SIG funding draw-down approved within a fortnight. The replacement cubicles delivered an immediate reduction in hock injuries — Robert's vet recorded a 40% drop in hock-related calls in the first three months. Lying-time monitoring (CowManager collars) showed an average increase from 11.2 to 12.6 hours per day.

For Robert, the hidden win was the install staging. Because we worked in rotating blocks rather than emptying the shed, he didn't lose a single milking — and didn't have to bed cows on straw in the yard, which would have cost him roughly £180 a day.

## Lessons for other dairies

Three things worth flagging if you're planning a similar job. Start the SIG paperwork twelve weeks before you want to pour — the capacity calc, exposure-class certificate and method statement all take time, and the fund won't release until they're approved. Plan your access road for 32-tonne deliveries even if your daily milk traffic is lighter; we've seen too many jobs delayed by a single soft patch on the haulage route. And if you're swapping cubicles in an occupied shed, talk to your installer early about block-rotation — it's harder to plan but it saves the cost of a temporary straw yard.
